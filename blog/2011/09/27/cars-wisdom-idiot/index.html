The Wayfarer.

On cars, wisdom and being an idiot

Guess what? I bought a car. It doesn’t have a roadworthy, isn’t registered, and doesn’t reverse. There we are above. Me and my piece of shit–my new pal. I think it’s pretty cool.

It’s a Mazda 1500. A classic car from 1969. Designed by Italian Giorgetto Giugiaro for Bertone. Legend goes that Alfa rejected Bertone’s design, so Bertone sold it to Mazda. Old men get all excited when they hear “Italian designed, Japanese made.” Personally, I like the windows.

I took it to a mechanic in Collingwood called Little Sam’s. The place belongs in an episode of Twin Peaks. Car parts scattered about. Guys with greasy hands. It lives up to my idea of a good, noble mechanic, at least aesthetically.

A quick jump start. A five minute drive. A park out the front.

Blue overalls says,

“Who’s Fiat is this?”

“Oh, not a Fiat. Mazda.”

“It’s in a really bad spot. Can you move it?”

“It doesn’t reverse.”

“Then push it.”

So we pushed it around the corner and I left them to get it into reverse. I walked to work with crossed fingers, knowing my piece of shit might soon be less of a piece of shit. But how much will it cost? How long will it take?

Am I an idiot?

Harumpf.

- - -

I’ve never been a car person.

In school I’d feel left out when the other boys talked about cars. But it didn’t bother me too much. Cars were for trouble-makers and kids with learning difficulties. I didn’t need cars as much as I didn’t need to listen to metal, because those things were not me things, or for me people.

Now I think differently. Cars, and their inner-workings, represent a series of values and lessons I’m still getting my head around. Things like lateral-thinking, patience, stamina, decision-making, learning a language, problem-solving, being frugal, working with others, confidence and identity. It’s a framework for learning about life.

I’ve never read Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance, but I imagine it’s the same thing. I bought a copy on Amazon. It’s sitting on my table at home.

- - -

Two days later, I walked to Little Sam’s during my lunch break.

“I dropped off my Mazda the other day. How’d you go?”

“Your gear shift linkage is completely worn. The selector just can’t make its way around.”

“Oh.”

“We made some calls. Can’t find a replacement part.”

“Oh.”

“If you find one at a wrecker we’ll install it for you.”

“Oh.”

I walked away and uncrossed my fingers. I had a feeling this might happen.

Am I an idiot?

- - -

You know what? Who cares! An idiot wonders if others think he’s an idiot, idiot.

I once had a chat to a guy named Scott Thomas. He was the lead designer on the Obama campaign in 2008. I published it as an interview on Junior. He said,

“A plant doesn’t grow in a set path. It grows based on where the sun is, how much rain it gets, how much water it receives, how much attention it is having. All these elements play into the plant’s growth. It applies the same to us. You don’t know which way your leaves are going to grow until they grow that way. It’s important to respect that, appreciate that, and to not look too far ahead into things.

Often times we want to have the answer to where we’re bound to go, but the truth is, we’re just here. If you spend your entire life living in the future you’re never going to enjoy the present and you’re never going to enjoy now. Then when all of a sudden you don’t have anything in the future to look towards, you don’t know how to appreciate the present. You become a sad old man. So I would suggest that everyone lives like a plant.”

Kinda esoteric, but it makes sense. This car is teaching me to be a plant. To focus on the task. To care less. To enjoy the present.

It’s working too. I’m coming to terms with my own self-doubt and building confidence to finish a project and finish it well. Soon it’ll get a roadworthy and I’ll drive it to the beach. Soon.

Oh god. We’re so cheesy. Me and my piece of shit. My new pal.
